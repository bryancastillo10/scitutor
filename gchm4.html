Ion Product of Water

The property of water to be amphoteric causes its interaction with another water molecule to undergo auto-ionization. This effect happens when water molecules exchange their protons with one another and it only happens at a very small extent. In this case, one molecule of water act as a Bronsted-Lowry acid (proton donor) while the other water molecule is the Bronsted-Lowry base (proton acceptor). To show the process, the chemical equation for it is:

H2O(l) + H2O(l) ⇌ H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq), or can be simplified to
2H2O(l) ⇌ H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)

With that chemical reaction at equilibrium, the equilibrium constant designated as Kw with a value of 1.00 x 10-14 at room temperature (25°C). The equilibrium expression is:

Kw= [H3O+][OH-]= 1.00 x 10-14

Solving for the concentrations of hydronium (H3O+) and hydroxide (OH-) ions, we have:

1.00 x 10-14  =  [H3O+][OH-]   =   x2
x= √(1.00 x 10-14)   =   1.00 x 10-7
[H3O+]   =   [OH-]   =   1.00 x 10-7 M

It shows that the dissociated water molecules is very small and it is only applicable at the state of pure water molecule. Changes happen when [H3O+] > [OH-] by which the solution is acidic while [H3O+] < [OH-], the solution is basic. If the temperature changes, like other equilibrium constants, the value changes for auto-ionization of water. Kw ranges from 1.15 x 10-15 at 0°C to 4.99 x 10-13 at 100°C.

Hydrogen Bonding of Water

Water molecule is generally composed of two hydrogen atoms bonded with an oxygen atom. The bonds that hold those atoms together to form water molecule is through hydrogen bonding. It is a special type of dipole-dipole interaction by which it is a weak interaction. Between water molecules, hydrogen atom has a partial positive charge to attract with an electronegative atom such as O resulting for each water molecules to bonded with each other. The hydrogen atom exhibits partial positive charge while oxygen atom, a more electronegative atom exhibits partial negative charge making it to have a property of being polar.

Acid-Base Equilibria

Acids and bases have their own strength variety that signifies how much they are capable to donate or accept protons. For strong acids and strong bases, they are capable of complete ionization and thus their equilibrium constants are relatively large. For weak acids and weak bases, they have their equilibrium constants vary greatly depending also on how much proton they can donate or accept. These constants are relatively small in quantity and represented by KA is for equilibrium constant for acid while KB is equilibrium constant for base. In any acid-base reaction, the equilibrium will favor the reaction that moves the proton to the stronger base. We can illustrate the equilibrium constants of acids and bases as follows:

Hypothetical acid in water equilibrium:

HA   +   H2O ⇌   H3O+   +   A−
KA =
[H3O+][A−] [HA]

Hypothetical base in water equilibrium:

A−   +   H2O ⇌   HA   +   OH−
KB =
[HA][OH−] [A−]

The relationship between KA and KB can be illustrated by multiplying the two expressions:

KAKB =
([H3O+][A−] [HA])([HA][OH−] [A−])
KAKB = [H3O+][OH-]

Remember that: KW = [H3O+][OH-], so:

KAKB= KW

This derived equation can be used to correlate KA and KB such that Kw is given at certain temperatures and also indicates that they are inversely proportional to each other.

Diprotic and Polyprotic Acids and Bases

There are acids that can give more than one proton upon their ionization. Thus, they have two or more equilibrium expressions and constants to illustrate their acidity. It is always the first proton than any of the successive proton easier to be removed and donate to the base. For example, we have carbonic acid (H2CO3) at 25°C which is a diprotic acid.

First proton dissociation:

H2CO3 + H2O ⇌ HCO3− + H3O+
KA1 =
[H3O+][HCO3−] [H2CO3]= 4.45 x 10− 7

Second proton dissociation:

HCO3− + H2O ⇌ CO32− + H3O+
KA2 =
[H3O+][CO32−] [HCO3−]= 4.69 x 10− 11

It shows that the value of KA successively becomes smaller in value as protons get donated. It is become less favorable for the acid to donate the proton as it approaches its form with negative ion. The trend of their values should be KA1 > KA2 > and so on. This step by step donation of proton indicates that the acid molecule becomes less acid when protons are lost. On the other hand, polyprotic bases has the same trend and principle of designating KB values such that bases is for accepting protons. For example, the carbonate ion (CO32−) at 25°C is illustrated:

First proton acceptance:

CO32− + H2O ⇌ HCO3− + OH−
KB1 =
[OH−][HCO3−] [CO32−]= 2.13 x 10− 4

Second proton acceptance:

HCO3− + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 + OH−
KB2 =
[OH−][H2CO3] [HCO3−]= 2.25 x 10− 8

Common Ion Effect

In acid-base equilibrium, there are cases by which two soluble solutes can contain the same ion (either cation or anion) by which it is termed as the common ion. With this situation of the presence of common ions, there is a suppresion on the strength on ionization of either the weak acid or the weak base. For example, we have two reactions in a solution which are sodium acetate, NaCH3COO, and acetic acid, CH3COOH. It has a common acetate ion, CH3COO−, upon its dissociation.

NaCH3COO → CH3COO− + Na+
CH3COOH ⇌ CH3COO− + H+

Observing each of the component, NaCH3COO is a strong electrolyte while CH3COOH is a weak acid. As a result, the common ions from NaCH3COO suppreses the ionization of CH3COOH making it less acidic than the usual. This event is called the common ion effect. It is the shift in equilibrium due to the addition of a common ion with the ionized substance. Due to the suppresion of sodium acetate in the example, the equilibrium of acetic acid shifts from right to left side of the reaction. Common ions also greatly affect solubility of salts. Addition of common ion decreases the solubility of a salt which also shifts the equilibrium from right to left side of the reaction. The resullt is precipitation reaction following the Le Chatelier's principle on managing chemical stresses.Another important thing in common ion effect is that it plays an vital role in determination of the pH of a solution. The general expression for the pH of such solutions is governed by Henderson-Hasslebalch Equation.

pH = pKA + log
[Conjugate Base Acid]

In the example between sodium acetate and acetic acid solution, we can express the determination of its pH by Henderson-Hasslebalch equation as follows:

pH = pKA + log
[CH3COO− CH3COOH]

This equation is also applicable to special solutions called buffer. A buffer is a solution between a weak acid or weak base with its salt. It has the ability to resist changes of pH whenever there is adjustments through addition of small amounts of acid or base. This characteristic of buffer to resist pH changes is very crucial to some chemical and biological systems. For example, the buffering system in the blood which involves carbonic acid,H2CO3, and bicarbonate ion,HCO3−.

Whenever a base entered the bloodstream, carbonic acid reacts with the hydroxide ion yielding bicarbonate ions and water. Meanwhile, when an acidic substance enters into the bloodstream, bicarbonate ions reacts with hydronium ions forming carbonic acid and water. Both carbonic acid and bicarbonate ions are natural components in the bloodstream, thus making the products on the reaction with them neutral and would not affect the pH of the system. The pH of blood is critical to the lives of the organism such as humans and sudden change of it can result to death. pH of the blood is ranging from 7.35 to 7.45 which is classified as slightly alkaline.

For addition of base:

H2CO3 + OH− → HCO3− + H2O

For addition of acid:

HCO3− + H3O+ → H2CO3 + H2O

  2. What is the pH of a buffer solution consists of 0.500 M acetic acid and 0.200 M sodium acetate? KA for acetic acid = 1.75 x 10− 5

    1. Identify the equilibrium reaction between the dissociation of dichloroacetic acid:

      Cl2HC2OOH ⇌ Cl2HC2OO− + H+

      Use ICE table to derive the equilibrium expression, let x= 0.0070 since [H+]=0.0070 M which is given in the problem:

      Chemical Equation: Cl2HC2OOH ⇌ Cl2HC2OO− + H+
      I 0.100 M 0 0
      C − 0.0070 + 0.0070 + 0.0070
      E 0.100 − 0.0070 0.0070 M 0.0070 M

      The equilibrium expression is:

      KA =
      (0.0070)2 (0.100 M − 0.0070 M)=4.9 x 10− 5 0.093
      =5.3 x 10− 4

    2. You can also use Henderson Hasslebalch formula between a salt and an weak acid with a common ion effect.

      pH = pKA + log
      [[salt] [acid]
      pH = −logKA + log
      [[NaCH3COO] [CH3COOH]
      pH = −log(1.75 x 10− 5) + log
      (0.200 M 0.500 M)
      pH = 4.36

Introduction to Electrochemistry

There is a field in chemistry that focuses on the chemical processes involved to yield electricity. That study is called electrochemistry. Electricity is generated through the sudden movement of electrons from one element into another and that chemical reaction is called redox reaction. Redox reaction is the term to emphasize reduction and oxidation chemical reactions which always occur simultaneously. Whenever redox reaction occur, the oxidation state of one element changes. Reduction is the process of chemical species gaining an electron (reduce in oxidation number) while oxidation is the process of losing an electron (increase in oxidation number). Those two reactions can be illustrated by two half cell reactions and then when these two are combined forms the overall reaction. Let's take the example between zinc (Zn) and copper (Cu) that undergoes redox reaction.

Reduction: Cu(aq)2+ + 2e− → Cu(s)
Oxidation: Zn(s) → Zn(aq)2+ + 2e−

Those are the half cell reactions between zinc and copper redox reaction by which solid zinc undergoes oxidation while copper ion is for reduction. You can observe that copper gains an electron (reduction) and then zinc loses an electron (oxidation). Combining the two half cell reactions by just adding each of them and then the term 2e− is omitted, we have:

Cu(aq)2+ + Zn(s) → Zn(aq)2+ + Cu(s)

This is the overall reaction of the redox reaction by which reduction and oxidation is both represented in the reaction. To better remember the principle of redox reactions you can follow the mnemonic "OIL RIG". OIL stands for oxidation is lose of electron while RIG stands for reduction is gain of electron. Observing the half cell reactions of redox reaction the term that represents the electron (e−) is \ always at the reactant side for the reduction while at the product side for the oxidation.

Since this reactions always consist of two half cell reactions, the question is which of them is most probably to undergo reduction and the other for oxidation? The way to identify it by determining their potential (E°) in a standard reduction table which consists of the reduction potentials of every possible half cell reactions. The higher value of their potential between the two half cell reaction has to undergo reduction while the other half cell is for the oxidation of the redox reaction.

There are many possible half cell reactions which has its respective standard potential and from the table are some of it. Looking back from the previous example of redox reaction which are between zinc and copper, you can see the values of their half cell reactions. Copper (E°= 0.337 V) has greater potential than zinc (E°= − 0.763) which signifies that copper half cell reaction is more capable to proceed reduction while zinc is for oxidation in this case.

Application of Electrochemistry

Redox reactions is commoly applied to the formation of electrical cells. These electrical cells has its function to convert energies in order to either store them or use them in our daily activities. The two general cells are classified voltaic cell(galvanic cell) and electrolytic cell. In these cells, there are electrodes which represents the redox reaction that takes place. Cathode is for reduction while anode is for oxidation. You can differentiate each of them with respect to its properties. As shown the table below are the general comparison between the two types of electrical cells:

Galvanic Cell Electrolytic Cell
Converts chemical energy into electrical energy Converts electrical energy into chemical energy
Redox reaction is spontaneous Redox reaction is nonspontaneous(Electrical current is passed through the cell by an external voltage)
Sign Conventions: Cathode (+ electrode), Anode (− electrode) Sign Conventions: Cathode (− electrode), Anode (+ electrode)
The movement of electron is from anode to cathode The movement of electron is from cathode to anode
Common example of galvanic cell are household batteries Common example of electrolytic cell is the deposition of metals by electroplating
