Acids and Bases

A special type of chemical reaction occurs whenever there are pairs of acids and base. They are known to be classification of chemicals or substances through the pH scale. Acids and bases can be defined with three different theories which are the Arrhenius theory, Bronsted-Lowry theory, and Lewis theory. They have different definitions on an acid and a base but they still reflect the same chemical reaction occured and the reacting species of the acids and bases. To further understand an acid and a base, here is a hypothetical reaction between an acid and base with its parts:

HX + H2O ⇌ H3O+ + X-

In this representation: HX is the acid, H2O is the base, H3O+ is the conjugate acid, and X- is the conjugate base. In addition, a proton of the acid can either be represnted by H+ or H3O+.

Acids and bases were also categorized depending on strengths on how acidic or how basic they could be. This can be identified through the scale called pH. However, there are times the value of pH is not given. There is still a way to determine the strength of acids and bases. They can be described as follows:

  • Strong Acid- all acid halides are considered to be strong acids except for HF (due to the hydrogen bonding). These includes HCl, HBr, and HI. Other strong acids include nitric acid(HNO3), sulfuric acid (H2SO4), and perchloric acid (HClO4).
Theories Definition of Acids Definition of Bases Example of Acid-Base Pairs
Ahrrenius Theory contains H+ contains OH- HCl (acid) and NaOH (base)
Bronsted-Lowry Theory Proton Donor Proton Acceptor H2O (acid) and NH3 (base)
Lewis Theory Electron Pair Acceptor Electron Pair Donor BF3 (acid) and NH3 (base)
  • Weak Acid- almost organic acids are considered to be weak acids such as acetic acid (CH3COOH) and formic acid (CHCOOH). Other weak acids are phosphoric acid (H3PO4) and hydrogen sulfide (H2S)
  • Strong Bases-almost strong bases are those who has hydroxides (OH-) as their component. Some strong bases include sodium hydroxide (NaOH), potassium hydroxide (KOH), and calcium hydroxide Ca(OH)2
  • Weak Bases-almost organic bases like pyridine (C5H5N) and trimethylamine (CH3)3N and also ionic bases such as HS- and HCOO-. Another weak base is ammonia (NH3)

The pH Scale

Whenever discussing about acids and bases, the parameter pH measures the acidity or alkalinity (basicity) of a substance. The pH scale has a value ranging from 0 to 14 wherein 7 is the midpoint that represents the neutral condition. If pH approaches the ranges below 7, the substance is acidic while if the pH is from 7 above up to 14, the substance is basic. pH can be represented mathematically as the negative logarithm of H+ ions (p-function of H+ ions):

pH = −log[H+]   or   [H+] = 10−pH

Water(H2O) is a special molecule with regards to pH because it has the property of being ampotheric. This property is defined as water could either act as an acid or base with its interaction with other molecules. Ampotherism is greatly depends on the oxidation states of the oxides available in a molecule. Another thing is that, pH is a very important factor for determining quality of the water. Theoretically, water at absolute condition has a pH of 7 (neutral condition). However, pH changes relatively due to the chemical constituents involved or its temperature. Usually, at lower temperature, the pH rises up (pH > 7) approaching basic condition while at higher temperature, pH of water becomes acidic (pH < 7). As stated there are also chemical constituents that makes water changes its pH. In depth discussion on acid-base reactions are further discussed on analytical chemistry section.

Another notation that can be expressed in acid-base reactions is the pOH scale. This scale is the p-function of hydroxide (OH-) ions which is also related to the pH scale. It is similar to pH but this time it focuses on the hydroxide concentration:

pOH   =   −log[OH−]   or   [OH−] = 10−pOH

The relationship between pH and pOH at room temperature is:

pH   +   pOH   =   14

Introduction to Chemical Equilibrium

In some chemical reactions, there is a state by which both the reactants and products have no further susceptibility to changes as each of the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant at any time. This concept of balanced forward and reverse reaction is called chemical equilibrium. A chemical reaction at equilibrium state has always arrow representation of ⇌ which indicates the reaction could either proceed in a forward or reverse direction in order to conserve the equal concentrations of reactants and products. Chemical equlibrium is further supported by Le Chatelier's Principle by which it states that if an external stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system adjusts in such a way that the stress is partially offset as it tries to re-establish equilibrium.. Consider the hypothetical reaction:

A + 2B ⇌ AB2

To correlate the concentrations of each reactants and product, the term equilibrium constant (Kc)is expressed. The expression Kc is always represented by the ratio of the concentrations at reverse rate over the concentrations at forward rate of the reaction. It is illustrated as (based from the given hypothetical reaction):

Kc =
[AB2] [A][B]2

The numerator represents the product while the denominator represents the reactants. It also shows that coefficients involved in the chemical reaction affects the concentration of the specific component with coefficient exponentially. Concentration should be raised to 2 as expressed. Equilibrium expressions is also dependent to which phases are involved in the chemical reaction. There are two general classifications of chemical equilibrium which are homogenous equilibrium and heterogeneous equilibrium. Homogeneous equilibrium applies to reactions in which all reacting species are in the same phase. Meanwhile, heterogeneous equilibrium is for reactions involving reactants and products at different phases. To be familiarized with equilibrium expressions, there are general rules in writing the proper equilibrium constant expressions.

Writing Equilibrium Constant Expressions

  1. The concentrations of the reacting species in a chemical equilibrium can be expressed as:
    • For condensed phase: moles per Liter (M)
    • For gaseous phase: moles per Liter (M) or by pressure (usually in atm or mmHg )
  2. Pure solids and liquids (heterogeneous equilibria) and solvents (homogeneous equilibria) do not appear in the equilibrium constant expression
    3. Example:
    CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO3(s) + CO2(g)

    The partial equilibrium expression is:

    K'c =
    [CaO][CO2] [CaCO3]which should be simplified to:

    Kc= [CO2]
  3. Equilibrium constant (Kp or Kc) is dimensionless
  4. Specify the balanced chemical equation and the temperature in quoting a value for the equilibrium constant. The value of equilibrium constant of a reaction changes with respect to temperature that is why it is important to add temperature on the details.

Le Chatelier's principle is analogous to the mechanism of a seesaw. Whenever there is an applied weight (representing the chemical stress) to it, the balance gets interrupted. Adjustments must be done to maintain balance (condition of chemical equilibrium) and through that principle, certain steps are to be applied in order to bring back its balance.

The Form of K in Chemical Equilibrium

For gaseous phase reactions, the form of K is proportional to the pressures exerted by the reacting species. It is represented by Kp by which you can correlate it to the equilibrium constant in terms of molar concentration, Kc. It is mathematically represented as shown below where R is the gas constant (0.0821 L · atm/mol · K) , T is the absolute temperature (units in K), and Δn which is the change in moles (moles of products minus moles of reactants of condensed and gaseous phases only):

Kp=Kc(RT)Δn

Another scenario for the equilibrium constant if the whole sequence of chemical equation is change (either the reactants and products were swapped or balancing the chemical equation with different sequence of coefficients). Each of these has guidelines to follow:

  • Case 1: When the equation for a reversible reaction is written in the opposite direction, the Kc becomes the reciprocal of the original equilibrium constant

    • Example:

    N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g) at 25°C, Kc= 4.63 x 10-3

    Kc =
    [NO2]2 [N2O4]= 4.63 x 10-3

    When the chemical reaction is reversed,

    2NO2(g) ⇌ N2O4(g) at 25°C, K'c

    K'c =
    1 4.63 x 10-3= 216

  • Case 2:The value of K also depends on how the equilibrium equation is balanced.

    • Example:

    N2O4(g) ⇌ 2NO2(g) at 25°C, Kc= 4.63 x 10-3

    When the coefficient is changed,

    1 2N2O4 ⇌ NO2   at 25°C, K'c

    K'c= √ (Kc)= √ (4.63 x 10-3)= 0.0680

    Another quantity that can be obtained from chemical equilibrium is the reaction quotient (Qc). It is obtained by substituting the initial concentrations into the equilibrium constant expression instead of the original equilibrium concentrations. This value is an effective determinant to predict which side of the reaction is favorable through their comparison with the equilibrium constants.

    CasesPredictionCondition
    Qc < Kc Forward Reaction
    • Initial concentrations are too small
    • Consuming reactants to form products
    Qc > Kc Reverse Reaction
    • Initial concentrations are too large
    • Consuming products to form reactants
    Qc = Kc No Favorable Side of the Reaction
    • The system is at equilibrium

    Calculations Involved in Chemical Equilibrium

    There are cases that chemical equilibrium were interfered by a chemical stress, it results to the concentration of the reacting species to change. A known way to identify the equilibrium concentrations and analyze the chemical equilibrium to such stress is through the ICE table method. It is a modified and convenient method based on the principle of chemical equilibrium of charge and mass balance (on analytical chemistry) by which equilibrium expressions were derived. In the ICE table, I stands for initial concentration, C stands for the change in the concentration (the chemical stress), and E stands for equilibrium concentration. To use this table, you will construct a table where the initials of ICE as the rows while each of the reacting species as the columns of the table. Calculations with this techique is also applicable to acid-base equilibria. Consider again the hypothetical reaction for the equilibrium:

    A + 2B ⇌ AB2
    Chemical Equation: A + 2B ⇌ AB2
    I A B AB2
    C -x -2x +x
    E A-x B-2x AB2+ x

    Analyzing the table, you can observe that the row I shows the initial concentration which is the reacting species as is. On the other hand, through the change x (represented by row C), chemical stress was applied in terms of concentration change. The change greatly depends on the coefficient of the reacting species. Additionally, the portion on the reactants relatively show a negative sign which means it is being consumed while the product side is positive showing it is being generated throughout the change. Adding the components of each columns on rows I and C results to the formulation of the expressions in row E. Row E represents the equilibrium concentration, thus the resulting equilibrium expression is:

    Kc =
    [AB2+x] [A-x][B-2x]

    Sample Problem on Chemical Equilibrium

    Calcium carbonate decomposes at high temperatures to give calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.

    CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g)

    Kp for this reaction is 1.16 at 800°C. A 5.00 L vessel containing 10 grams of CaCO3(s) was evacuated to remove the air sealed, and then heated to 800°C. Ignoring the volume occupied by the solid, what will be the mass of the solids in the vessel once equilibrium is reached?

    Listing down the given:

    • massCaCO3= 10 grams
    • T= 800°C + 273.15 K = 1073.15 K
    • Δn = 1 - 0= 1

    To solve for the masses of the solids (the sum of the masses of CaCO3 and mass of CaO), you need to identify first the moles of each of the solids and then convert it to grams by their molecular weights(stoichiometry). However, the given are Kp which represents carbon dioxide only and the initial mass of CaCO3. With this given, convert Kp to Kc using the equation for their conversion and obtain the initial concentration of CaCO3 by stoichiometry.


    Converting Kp to Kc:

    Kp=Kc(RT)Δn
    Kc =
    Kp (RT)Δn=1.16 [(0.0821 L·atm/mol·K)(1073.15 K)]1=0.01317

    Finding the initial concentration of CaCO3:

    molesCaCO3= (10 g CaCO3)
    (1 mol CaCO3 100 g CaCO3)=0.1 mol CaCO3

    To further visualize the problem, use ICE table method in the assessment of the chemical equilibrium

    MCaCO3=
    0.1 mol CaCO3 5 L=0.02 M

    Chemical Equation: CaCO3(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO2(g)
    I MCaCO3 0 0
    C -x +x +x
    E MCaCO3-x x x

    The equilibrium expression is: Kc= [CO2]= x = 0.01317

    The result of the chemical equilibrium can formulate as the equations for the cocentrations of CaCO3 and CaO. It shows:

    [CaCO3]= MCaCO3  − x =   0.02 M - 0.01317   =   6.83 x 10-3 M
    [CaO]   =   x   = 0.0137 M

    Now, getting the moles of both CaCO3 and CaO by the given volume:

    molesCaCO3=
    (6.83 x 10-3 moles CaCO3 L)(5 L)   = 0.03415 mol CaCO3
    molesCaO=
    (6.83 x 10-3 moles CaO) L)(5 L)   = 0.06585 mol CaO

    The last step is identifying the masses of each solid and then take their sum:

    massCaCO3= (0.03415 mol CaCO3)
    (100 g CaCO3 1 mol CaCO3)=   3.415 g CaCO3
    massCaO= (0.06585 mol CaO)
    (56 g CaO 1 mol CaO)=   3.688 g CaO

    Adding the mass of CaCO3 and CaO:

    Total mass of solids= 3.415 g CaCO3 + 3.688 g CaO = 7.1 grams solids

    Chemical Stresses

    Chemical stresses are the changes in the condition of the chemical equilibrium that results to interruption on the equilibrium and thus favoring one side of the reaction. Prediction of which side would be favorable after applying chemical stress is possible through Le Chatelier's principle. Here are the conditions that can be applied to a chemical equilibrium:

  • Change in the Concentration of the Reacting Species
    • Prediction Conditions
    Forward Reaction
  • Increase in the concentration of the reactants
  • Decrease in the concentration of the products
    		•
    Reverse Reaction
  • Increase in the concentration of the products
  • Decrease in the concentration of the reactants
    		•

  • Change in Pressure and Volume

    • This criteria is only applicable for reactions at gaseous phase. The greater the pressure, the smaller its volume which means that both pressure and volume are inversely proportional (based on Boyle's law).

    Prediction Conditions
    Forward Reaction
  • Decrease in the pressure of the system
  • Increase in the number of moles of the gases (increased volume)
    		•
    Reverse Reaction
  • Increase in the pressure of the system
  • Decrease in the number of moles of the gases (decreased volume)
    		•

  • Change in Temperature

    • This is the only factor that can changes the numerical value of the equilibrium constant. A temperature increase favors an endothermic reaction(ΔH=+) while a temperature decrease favors an exothermic reaction(ΔH=−).

    Prediction Conditions
    Forward Reaction
  • Endothermic Reaction

    • Example: N2O4 + heat → 2NO2   ΔH=+ kJ/mol
    Reverse Reaction
  • Exothermic Reaction

    • Example: N2O4 ← 2NO2 + heat   ΔH=− kJ/mol
