Lewis Structure

A very effective way to illustrate valence electrons in an element or a compound is through the illustration of Lewis structure. It is a simplified representation of the element wherein valence electrons are represented by dots and bonds are represented by a line. It also exhibits how bonding of molecules should follow the octet rule. Octet rule is the ability of atoms to achieve eight electrons in total as part of their valence shells which signifies their stability. Elements that already followed this rule are noble gases (group VIII in the periodic table). However, there are some molecules that have exceptions in octet rule which is to be discussed also in this section. Let's take as an example the drawing of Lewis structure of N2 molecule.

  • The total number of valence electron on one nitrogen atom is 5 e−. Since there are two atoms, we have 10 e− available.
  • Connecting two dots to represent a covalent bond (shows that two valence electrons share each other)
  • With that single bond formed, it still doesn't follow the octet rule by which each atom should have eight valence electrons, we need to add more bonds until it meets the octet rule.
  • Addition of two more bonds that makes N2 a triple bond shows that each atom follows the octet rule. Counting the valence electrons (1 bond = 2 valence electrons that are shared to each other), the total number of valence electron is still 10 e−. Thus, the appropriate Lewis structure for N2 is the one with triple bond.

How to Draw a Lewis Structure of Molecules

  1. Determine the elements present in the molecule and valence electrons of each. Take the sum of it.
  2. Place the each elements together wherein the central atom is at the center. The central atom is the one that can form the greatest number of bonds with respect to their valence electrons.
  3. You can now add the electrons represented by dots in pairs surrounding the atoms. A line represents a covalent bond between the atoms. Follow the octet rule as much as possible.
  4. If there are remaining elecrons, it signifies that there is a formal charge. It should be indicated in the Lewis structure at the upper right side of the molecule with brackets enclosed the structure.

Fomal Charges

Through the Lewis structure, you can determine how many valence electrons are present for a compound to be formed. Additionally, constructing a Lewis structure is more appropriate when you know the formal charge of an atom in the molecule.Formal charge is the charge of an atom in a molecule or a polyatomic ion when all of the bonding electrons that were shared equally. To determine the formal charge,

Formal Charge of an Atom = Number of Valence e− − e− Surrounding the Atom

such that

e− Surrounding the Atom = Lone Pair e− +
Bonding e− 2

As you can observe from the formula for electrons surrounding the atom, bonding e− is divided by two because in calculating formal charges, we only consider the electrons WITHIN the atom itself and disregard the shared electron from bonding. Deviations in Octet Rule

Octet rule is most applicable on elements with atomic number below 20 by which they comply their stability by achieving eight valence electrons to give the same electronic configurations as of the noble gases. However, there are cases some elements that doesn't require the octet rule when forming stable bonds with another atom.

  1. Reduced Octet Rule

    2. This case happens when there are too few valence electrons involved to complete the total of eight valence electron in an atom. It is common with the bonding of elements such as boron, aluminum, and beryllium. For example, we have borane (BH3) and Beryllium chloride (BeCl2).

  2. Odd Number of Electrons Compounds

    3. This exception is for cases when the total number of valence electrons of a molecule is equal to an odd number. Valence electrons usually come in pairs with regards to its sharing to the other atom and its polarity (represented by lone pairs of electrons). However, there are highly reactive chemical species called free radicals that are formed from the odd number of total valence electrons. The reason for them to be highly reactive they want to take an electron to another molecule in order for them to become stable. It is very rare to find radicals that are stable enough just like nitric oxide (NO) which has odd number of valence electrons (11 in total). To indicate unstable radicals, you can put "·" symbol beside on the chemical formula. For example, hydroxyl radical is represented by ·OH.   3. Expanded Octet Rule

    4. Other than the case of too few valence electrons, there is also a situation wherein valence electrons are too many that exceeds the eight electrons to follow octet rule. This is usually happening with molecules with highly electronegative terminal atoms (atoms that surrounds the central atom). Examples of molecules under the expanded octet rule are phosphorus pentachloride, PCl5 (total valence electron of 40 e−) and xenon tetrafluoride, XeF4 (total valence electron of 36 e−). The reason for this event is that there is an empty d orbitals (in the third period elements like P) for them to allow excess electrons to occupy thus making chemical bonds. Bonding Theories

Seeking for the full understanding of chemical bonding, there are several theories that were derived to demonstrate how valence electrons behave as it undergoes chemical bonding. These theories are acceptable in different situations that further reveals the identity and nature of some molecules.

Valence Bond Theory

This bonding theory states that only valence orbitals participate in the formation of a bond. The process of forming this bonds is called hybridization by which interaction of the valence electrons of participating atoms in the bonding forms a hybrid atomic orbital. For example, we have the bonding between carbon atoms in single bond (C-C bond) which forms a sp3 hybridized orbital.

This diagram shows that the valence electrons of carbon forms a hybrid orbital indicated by 2s,2px,2py, and 2pz which is an example of sp3 orbital. They are distribute eqaully to form a single bond. Other hybrids that can be formed are sp2 (example is from carbon-carbon double bond), sp( example is from carbon-carbon from triple bond), sp3d and sp3d2 (formed from expanded octet rule).

VSEPR Theory

VSEPR which stands for "Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion" Theory is a bonding theory that focuses on predicting the shape or geometry of the molecule with assuming that the valence electrons are responsible for their framework. Geometries can be classified into two groups which are electronic geometry (in terms of arrangement of electron groups) and molecular geometry (in terms of arrangement of atoms in three-dimensional space). These two geometries follow the repulsion strength according to VSEPR theory that states that Bonding Pair − Lone Pair is less than Lone Pair − Lone Pair relationship. Molecular Orbital Theory

The last bonding theory to be discussed is the molecular orbital theory. It demonstrates the mixing of atomic orbitals with the concept of the orbital as a wavefunction. Bonding orbitals are the constructive interferences while antibonding-orbitals represent the destructive interferences of a wave. To illustrate molecular orbital diagrams, it is a linear combination of atomic orbitals. Molecular orbital diagram is the most effective way to show properties such as bond orders,paramagnetism, or diagmagnetic property of a molecule. Consider the molecular orbital diagram of H2 molecule:

As you can observe in illustrating molecular orbital diagram, atomic orbital on the sides is for the individual H atom while at the center are the mixed orbitals (bonding orbitals at the bottom and anti-bonding orbitals at the top). Filling up of the electrons still follows the fundamuntal rules such as Aufbau principle, Pauli exclusion, and Hund's rule. Lower energy levels are the bonding orbitals (σ) and anti-bonding orbitals (σ*) represents a higher energy level. With this separation, you can now determine the property called bond order. It is a form of measurement that identifies the number of electrons involved in a bond between two atoms in a molecule. It is a good indicator for bond stability by evaluating the molecular orbital diagram. The formula for finding bond order with M.O. theory is:

Bond Order =
1 2( Total e−   at σ orbital − Total e−   at σ* orbital)

Another property that can be identified in M.O. theory is paramagnetism or diamagnetism of a molecule. As discussed on the lesson on electronic configurations, diamagnetism is exhibited on paired electrons while paramagnetism is on unpaired electrons. As the valence electrons on the molecular orbital diagram is shown, you can determine if the molecule is paramagnetic or diamagnetic.
